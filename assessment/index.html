How we assess

The assessment produces an explanation, not a stack of results.

We begin with diagnosis, safety, function and chronology. We then map the processes beneath the syndrome: how threat, reward, sleep, physiology, metabolism, cognition and context are behaving in this person. Tests are selected when the result can identify a medical contributor, clarify a hypothesis, establish a useful baseline or change treatment.

Assessment domains

Clinical & psychiatric

Onset, course, symptom pattern, impairment, risk, previous treatment, medication effects, development, trauma, relationships and current demands. This creates the clinical pattern that later findings must help explain.

Learning & psychological function

Threat learning, reward, attention, avoidance, emotion regulation, attachment, mentalization and coping. These processes influence how situations and bodily signals are interpreted and acted upon.

Sleep & autonomic regulation

Sleep timing and continuity, breathing, heart-rate and blood-pressure patterns, orthostatic symptoms and recovery. Together, they show how arousal is regulated across the day, during sleep and under demand.

Metabolic, immune & endocrine

Glucose and lipid regulation, inflammatory signals, thyroid and sex hormones, nutritional status and medication-related effects can influence energy, cognition, mood and recovery. They provide context; they are not psychiatric diagnoses.

Physical capacity & recovery

Activity, cardiorespiratory fitness, body composition and recovery show how the body responds to sustained demand. More specialized measures are considered only when they answer a defined question.

Cognition, neurophysiology & imaging

Objective testing separates attention, memory, processing speed and executive function. EEG, qEEG, imaging or specialist assessment are added when they can answer a neurological or treatment question that the clinical assessment cannot resolve alone.
