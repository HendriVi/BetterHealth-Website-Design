Scientific and clinical foundation

Psychiatric symptoms emerge from a brain regulating a body.

The brain does not passively record the world. It predicts what is likely to happen, interprets signals from inside the body, and prepares physiology before demands arrive. BetterHealth uses this science as a clinical framework: diagnosis defines the syndrome and risk, while deeper phenotyping asks which mechanisms may be maintaining it in this person.

Prediction, interoception and allostasis

Perception is an inference constrained by sensory evidence. The brain combines incoming information with prior learning and current context to estimate what a sensation means. Interoception is the same process applied to signals from within the body, including heartbeat, breathing, temperature, energy state, pain and visceral activity. A fast heartbeat can therefore be interpreted as exercise, excitement, illness or danger depending on the biological state and the model the brain has learned.

Allostasis is the brain's anticipatory regulation of the body. It coordinates autonomic, endocrine, immune, metabolic, motor and behavioral responses before and during demand. This is adaptive when the response is proportionate and resolves. Repeated activation without adequate recovery can become biologically expensive. Experimental work at cellular level shows that sustained glucocorticoid signaling can increase energy expenditure and accelerate markers of biological ageing. This does not reduce depression or anxiety to an energy deficit; it demonstrates that persistent stress regulation has measurable biological costs.

Foundational reading: Barrett & Simmons, 2015 [1]; Barrett, Quigley & Hamilton, 2016 [2]; Bobba-Alves et al., 2023 [3].

What this changes clinically

The same diagnosis can contain different mechanisms.

Two people can meet criteria for depression while differing in sleep architecture, threat learning, autonomic reactivity, endocrine state, inflammatory activity, metabolic health, medication effects, trauma history and social context. These differences matter because a treatment can be effective at group level while being biologically or psychologically mismatched to an individual.

A useful example comes from a randomized trial of the anti-inflammatory drug infliximab in treatment-resistant depression. The drug did not outperform placebo in the full sample. Among participants with higher baseline inflammatory markers, however, the pattern of response was different. The lesson is not that inflammation explains depression or that infliximab should be used routinely. It is that clinically similar syndromes can contain biologically distinct subgroups, and that a measurement becomes valuable when it changes a treatment hypothesis rather than merely producing more data.

Clinical example: Raison et al., 2013 [4].

Why selected modalities can work

Different interventions act on different parts of the system.

The scientific rationale does not determine treatment by itself. Clinical indication, safety, prior response, feasibility and the strength of evidence remain decisive. The descriptions below explain mechanisms and evidence without disclosing individual protocols or internal decision rules.

01 / Psychotherapy

Updating models through experience

Psychotherapy changes more than the story a person tells. It changes attention, interpretation, action and learning. In exposure-based work, a feared prediction is activated while the expected outcome fails to occur or becomes more manageable than anticipated. Repeated, well-designed experiences can reduce the certainty assigned to threat predictions and expand the range of situations the nervous system can classify as safe. Other therapies work through related processes: revising meanings, increasing behavioral flexibility, improving emotion regulation and changing interpersonal expectations. The technique matters, but so do the learning conditions in which it is delivered.

02 / Sleep and rhythm

Restoring temporal regulation

Sleep is an active biological process that influences synaptic plasticity, emotional memory, endocrine timing, immune signaling, glucose regulation and cognitive control. Treating insomnia can therefore improve more than sleep duration. In the OASIS randomized trial, digital cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia improved insomnia and also reduced paranoia and hallucination-related experiences. Circadian interventions can be equally powerful when timing is the relevant mechanism: a controlled trial found that appropriately timed bright-light therapy improved bipolar depression. Because light and sleep manipulation can also destabilize mood in susceptible patients, these are clinical interventions rather than generic wellness advice.

03 / HRV biofeedback

Training a physiological control loop

Heart-rate variability reflects variation in the intervals between heartbeats; it is not a simple score of health or vagal tone. During slow, individually calibrated breathing, respiratory and cardiovascular oscillations can become coupled near the body's baroreflex resonance. Biofeedback makes this normally hidden loop visible so that the person can learn to influence it. Experimental work has shown increases in baroreflex gain, and controlled studies report reductions in anxiety in some populations. We use HRV as contextual physiological information and a trainable regulatory signal, not as a stand-alone psychiatric diagnosis.

04 / Exercise and nutrition

Changing the biological conditions for brain function

Exercise is a repeated systems-level stimulus. It changes cardiorespiratory capacity, insulin sensitivity, vascular function, inflammatory signaling, skeletal-muscle metabolism and neurotrophic pathways while also providing behavioral activation and mastery. Randomized trials support exercise as a treatment component in depression. Nutrition can alter substrate availability, micronutrient status, glucose dynamics and inflammatory exposure. The SMILES trial showed that structured dietary improvement could produce clinically meaningful benefit in major depression. More intensive metabolic strategies, including ketogenic therapy, are scientifically interesting but remain condition-specific and less established; current psychiatric evidence includes promising pilot studies rather than definitive proof.

05 / Neurofeedback and stimulation

Targeting measurable neural dynamics

Neurofeedback presents a person with a real-time representation of selected brain activity and uses learning to increase voluntary influence over that signal. Proof-of-concept randomized work has shown that people with depression can learn to modulate amygdala-related activity, although protocols, targets and clinical evidence vary considerably. Non-invasive brain stimulation acts differently: repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation induces electrical currents in cortical tissue and can modify network excitability and connectivity. Large randomized trials support established stimulation protocols for depression. These technologies are selected for a defined clinical question; a colorful brain map alone does not establish a treatment target.

06 / Advanced adjuncts

Promising mechanisms require calibrated claims

Photobiomodulation uses red or near-infrared light to influence photochemical processes that may affect mitochondrial signaling, cerebral blood flow and inflammatory pathways. Small sham-controlled psychiatric trials are encouraging, but the evidence is still preliminary. Hyperbaric oxygen changes dissolved oxygen exposure and can influence vascular, inflammatory and repair signaling. Results depend strongly on indication and protocol: a recent sham-controlled trial reported benefit in combat-related PTSD, while an earlier rigorous trial found no benefit for persistent postconcussion symptoms. We therefore distinguish biological plausibility, early clinical signal and established efficacy rather than treating them as interchangeable.

Evidence calibration

A mechanism explains why something might work. A trial tests whether it does.

BetterHealth separates four questions that are often collapsed in health marketing: Is the proposed mechanism biologically coherent? Has the intervention changed the intended target in humans? Has it improved meaningful clinical outcomes under controlled conditions? Does the evidence apply to this individual, considering diagnosis, comorbidity, medication, contraindications and treatment history? A persuasive biological story cannot substitute for outcome data, and an average trial result cannot substitute for clinical judgment.

This is also why we do not order every available test. A measurement must answer a defined question, be interpretable in context, and have a plausible route to changing care. Biomarkers, wearables and neurophysiology can strengthen or weaken a hypothesis; none of them can replace the clinical encounter or diagnose a psychiatric disorder in isolation.
