The science behind the method

Mental health emerges from prediction, physiology and experience.

The brain interprets the world through prior learning, reads signals from inside the body and prepares physiology for what it expects next. BetterHealth uses this framework clinically. Diagnosis establishes the syndrome and risk; a more detailed assessment asks which processes are maintaining the symptoms in this person.

Prediction, interoception and allostasis

Perception is an inference constrained by sensory evidence. The brain combines what is arriving now with what it has learned before and with the current context. Interoception applies the same process to signals from within the body, including heartbeat, breathing, pain, temperature and energy state. The signal matters, but so does the model used to interpret it.

Allostasis is the brain's anticipatory regulation of the body. It coordinates autonomic, endocrine, immune, metabolic, motor and behavioral responses before and during demand. This is adaptive when the response resolves. Repeated activation without sufficient recovery has measurable biological costs; it does not, however, reduce depression or anxiety to a single bodily mechanism.

Foundational reading: Barrett & Simmons [1]; Barrett, Quigley & Hamilton [2]; Bobba-Alves et al. [3].

What this changes clinically

The same diagnosis can arise through different pathways.

Two people can meet criteria for depression while differing in sleep, threat learning, autonomic reactivity, endocrine state, inflammatory activity, metabolic health, medication effects, trauma history and social context. A treatment may work on average and still be poorly matched to the mechanism that matters most in one individual.

A randomized trial of infliximab in treatment-resistant depression illustrates the principle. The drug did not outperform placebo across the full group, but the response pattern differed among participants with higher inflammatory markers. This does not mean inflammation explains depression or that infliximab should be used routinely. It shows why a measurement is useful only when it changes a treatment hypothesis.

Our assessment therefore combines symptoms, chronology, function, learning history, examination and existing records. Selected measurements are added where they can distinguish between plausible explanations. The result states what is likely to matter, what remains uncertain and what should be addressed first.

Precision-treatment example: Raison et al. [4].

Why selected treatments can work

Different interventions act on different parts of the system.

Treatment is chosen for the clinical problem and proposed mechanism, not because a technology is available. Diagnosis, safety, previous response, feasibility and evidence determine whether a modality belongs in the plan.

01 / Psychotherapy

Changing predictions through experience

Psychotherapy changes attention, interpretation, action and learning. In exposure-based work, a feared prediction is activated while the expected outcome fails to occur or proves more manageable than anticipated. Repeated experience under the right conditions can reduce the certainty assigned to threat and expand behavioral flexibility.

02 / Sleep and rhythm

Restoring biological timing

Sleep shapes emotional memory, attention, endocrine timing, immune signaling, glucose regulation and cognitive control. Treating insomnia can therefore alter more than sleep duration. The OASIS trial found broader mental-health benefits from insomnia treatment, while controlled research supports correctly timed bright light in bipolar depression. Timing interventions require clinical caution because they can destabilize mood in susceptible people.

03 / HRV biofeedback

Training a physiological control loop

Heart-rate variability describes variation between heartbeats; it is not a simple score of health or vagal tone. During individually calibrated slow breathing, respiratory and cardiovascular oscillations can couple near baroreflex resonance. Biofeedback makes this normally hidden loop visible and trainable. HRV is used as contextual physiological information, not as a psychiatric diagnosis.

04 / Exercise and nutrition

Changing the conditions for brain function

Exercise changes cardiorespiratory capacity, insulin sensitivity, vascular function, inflammatory signaling and neurotrophic pathways while also providing behavioral activation. Randomized trials support exercise as part of depression treatment. Nutrition changes substrate availability, micronutrient status and glucose dynamics; the SMILES trial found benefit from structured dietary improvement. More intensive metabolic strategies remain less established and condition-specific.

05 / Neurofeedback and stimulation

Targeting measurable neural dynamics

Neurofeedback presents selected brain activity in real time and uses learning to increase voluntary influence over that signal. Early randomized work is promising, although protocols and evidence vary. Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation works differently: it changes cortical and network excitability, and established protocols are supported by large depression trials. A brain map alone does not establish a treatment target.

06 / Advanced adjuncts

Promising mechanisms require restrained claims

Photobiomodulation may influence mitochondrial signaling, cerebral blood flow and inflammation; small sham-controlled psychiatric studies are encouraging but preliminary. Hyperbaric oxygen can affect vascular, inflammatory and repair signaling, yet findings differ by indication and protocol. Biological plausibility, an early clinical signal and established efficacy are not interchangeable.

How we judge evidence

A mechanism explains why something might work. A trial tests whether it does.

We separate biological coherence, target engagement, controlled clinical outcomes and individual applicability. A persuasive mechanism cannot replace outcome data, and an average trial result cannot replace judgment about diagnosis, comorbidity, medication, contraindications and treatment history.

This is also why we do not order every available test. A measurement must answer a defined question, be interpretable in context and have a plausible route to changing care. Biomarkers, wearables and neurophysiology can support or weaken a hypothesis; none can diagnose a psychiatric disorder in isolation.
